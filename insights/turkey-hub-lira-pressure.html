Turkey as a hub: the limits of the lira route

Through the first half of 2026, Turkish banks have continued to tighten their internal limits on transactions with any Russia-related dimension. The trajectory is not new — it has been visible since the Treasury enforcement actions of late 2024 — but the scale has shifted. What was once a tactical narrowing is now structural, and the practical implication for practitioners is that Turkey has stopped being the elastic absorber of residual corridor traffic.

The mechanics of the tightening

The compliance posture at the larger Turkish private banks has, in effect, converged on the Western European standard. End-use review on dollar and euro flows has become the norm rather than the exception. Sanctioned-counterparty screening now extends to indirect ownership chains that, two years ago, would have been left at the level of the immediate party. KYC refresh on existing corporate relationships is being treated as a vehicle for offboarding rather than maintenance.

State and quasi-state institutions have moved more slowly but not in a different direction. Several have introduced internal exposure caps on counterparties from CIS jurisdictions, which has the effect of throttling new mandate volume even where existing relationships continue to operate.

The lira itself has continued under pressure through 2026 — driven by domestic monetary policy that has alternated between orthodoxy and accommodation without settling on either — and exporters quoting in lira are routinely demanding hedge premiums that erode the cost advantage Turkish routing once offered.

What still works

Turkey remains operationally indispensable for a specific set of flows.

First, physical logistics. Turkish freight forwarding, port handling, and overland trucking into the CIS continue to operate on a scale and at price points that no other regional jurisdiction matches. The settlement layer for these services has migrated; the services themselves have not.

Second, a narrowing band of small and mid-sized industrial trade where the Turkish counterpart is itself the manufacturer and the value chain does not need to traverse the international banking system in any significant way. This category was always the structural core of the Turkish corridor and remains so.

Third, certain consumer-goods re-export flows where the documentary path is straightforward and the value at risk is modest enough that smaller Turkish institutions remain willing to settle.

Where the volume is going

Most of the marginal mandate volume that left Turkey through Q4 2025 and Q1 2026 has migrated to the UAE. This is unsurprising — the dirham infrastructure absorbs almost any corridor flow that can be denominated in a freely convertible Gulf currency — but the speed of the migration has been notable. We have onboarded a meaningful number of mandates over the past six months whose primary banking relationship was, until recently, in Istanbul.

A smaller share has moved to Hong Kong correspondents working through Chinese banks with regional presence. This route is harder for Russian counterparties to access directly but works well where the underlying trade has a yuan-denominated leg.

What has not happened is any meaningful migration to Central Asia. Despite the policy framing of Almaty and Tashkent as alternatives, the banking sectors there have neither the depth nor the appetite to absorb material Turkey-displaced volume — a point we expand on in a separate note.

The takeaway

Turkey is no longer a flexible hub. It is a specialised hub, useful for specific kinds of trade and increasingly closed to others. Pricing the corridor today requires being explicit about which category a given mandate falls into. The reflexive assumption that lira routing is the cost-effective default no longer holds.
