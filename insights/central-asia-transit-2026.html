Central Asia transit: where the parallel-import corridor stands now

The Central Asian transit corridor — by which we mean primarily the Kazakhstan and Uzbekistan-routed flows of consumer electronics, industrial components, dual-use machinery, and selected automotive parts moving toward end-users in the CIS — has been under sustained external pressure for two years. The narrative, repeated annually in compliance briefings since 2024, is that the corridor is closing. It is not. But it is no longer the unconstrained transit channel it briefly was.

The compliance shift through 2025–2026

What changed materially over 2025 was the tooling on the Kazakh side. Mandatory end-use review on a defined list of HS codes — adopted by the larger Kazakh private banks in mid-2025 and extended in scope by a state-bank circular in early 2026 — has converted what was previously a documentary formality into a substantive due-diligence step. Importers are now required to demonstrate domestic Kazakh end-use of goods that have historically transited the country in volume.

In practice, the larger second-tier Kazakh banks now decline a meaningful percentage of corridor-pattern transactions outright. The smaller institutions still settle them, but at materially higher fees and with shorter operational windows.

Uzbekistan has been slower to adopt formal end-use frameworks but has tightened in a less visible way. Correspondent relationships with European banks, which Uzbek institutions have been actively building since 2023, have introduced compliance expectations that effectively constrain the corridor without an explicit local rule. The result is functionally similar to the Kazakh shift, just less legible from the outside.

What the corridor still does

The compressed corridor continues to handle three classes of flow.

First, established consumer-electronics and white-goods trade where the Central Asian importer has a credible domestic distribution footprint, and where a meaningful share of imported volume genuinely terminates locally. This category has always been the structural backbone of the corridor and remains so.

Second, industrial components and selected machinery where the documentation is supported by a real Central Asian end-use narrative. The volume here has thinned but the unit margins have widened.

Third, a residual flow of higher-risk categories that has migrated downmarket — to smaller banks, smaller intermediaries, and shorter settlement chains. This part of the corridor is the most exposed to further enforcement and, in our judgement, the least durable.

Currency mechanics

The dirham remains the dominant settlement currency for any leg that touches international banking. The yuan has grown materially through 2025, particularly for transactions where the original supplier is Chinese — Central Asian importers increasingly hold yuan accounts, and Chinese banks with regional presence are willing to settle directly. Tenge and som settlement work for the local end of the chain only; neither has emerged as a corridor-level unit of account.

Direct rouble settlement at the corridor level remains marginal, for the same liquidity reasons that constrain it elsewhere.

The takeaway

The Central Asia corridor today demands more rigorous structuring than it did eighteen months ago. The mandates that work are those where the end-use narrative is genuinely supportable, the counterparties have been screened against the new compliance posture, and the pricing reflects the compressed margins. Mandates structured on the assumption that Almaty or Tashkent will absorb anything sent their way are not a fit for current conditions.
