Iran corridor: how Gulf banking absorbs the residual flows

After the most recent round of US Treasury secondary-sanctions guidance — extended in early 2026 to cover a wider perimeter of Iranian petrochemical counterparties — what remains of operational Iran-linked trade has consolidated almost entirely into Gulf-routed structures denominated in dirham. The rial-settlement architecture announced over the past three years has, in practice, ceased to function as a primary settlement layer and now exists largely as a documentary fiction.

For practitioners, this is not a deterioration. It is a clarification.

The dirham as the actual unit of account

UAE banking depth, the dirham's de facto peg to the dollar, and the absence of Emirati exposure to secondary-sanctions enforcement have made dirham-denominated escrow the path of least friction for any transaction with an Iranian leg. The structure that has stabilised over the course of 2025 and into 2026 typically involves an Emirati intermediary holding funds against documentary triggers, with the Iranian counterpart accessing value through a separate, locally-cleared chain that does not touch the original transaction.

Operationally: the question of whether a counterpart “has rial liquidity” or “settles in rial” has become irrelevant for most structuring purposes. What matters is whether the counterpart can engage with a UAE-domiciled entity on an arm's-length basis, and whether the underlying commercial flow tolerates the documentary architecture this requires.

What the residual flow consists of

The operational flow that still moves is concentrated in three product categories.

First, petrochemicals — particularly methanol, urea, and the lighter end of the polymer slate — moving from Iranian refineries into Gulf-mediated channels for onward sale into South and East Asia. The buyers are not, in most cases, the original sanctioned end-users implied by political reporting; they are Asian processors with no direct sanctions exposure who source product on a delivered basis.

Second, metals — copper cathode, ferro-alloys, occasional aluminium — where the Iranian seller relies on Emirati commodity intermediaries to bridge the documentary gap. This trade has grown materially over the past eighteen months as competing supply from CIS sources has tightened.

Third, a smaller but persistent flow of consumer-staple imports into Iran, sourced from Turkish and South Asian manufacturers and routed via UAE distributors. The structuring on this leg is mature and unremarkable.

What does not work

Direct rial-denominated settlement at scale does not function. The Iranian banking system's external correspondent relationships have continued to thin, and the few institutions still maintaining limited Gulf relationships are reluctant to expand exposure. Any structure that requires the Iranian side to receive or hold value in a foreign-currency external account, rather than through a domestic-clearing surrogate, is generally not viable.

Equally, structures that route through Turkish banking — which functioned reasonably well for Iranian flows in 2022–2024 — have become substantially more difficult since Q4 2025. Turkish institutions have tightened their Iran-related limits in parallel with their Russia-related limits, and the practical effect is that Turkey is no longer the optional second route it was.

The takeaway

The Iranian corridor is now, structurally, a Gulf-mediated corridor. Practitioners working it should price the dirham infrastructure as the primary cost layer and treat anything described in rial as nominal. The political framework will continue to evolve; the operational plumbing has, for the moment, settled.
